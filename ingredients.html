Key French Ingredients

French butter, especially from Normandy or Brittany, is prized for its high fat content and rich, creamy flavor. Unsalted (doux) is preferred for cooking to control seasoning, while salted (demi-sel) is excellent for spreading. Look for European-style butter if specific French brands are unavailable.

A thick, tangy cultured cream with a rich flavor, similar to sour cream but less acidic and with a higher fat content, meaning it won't curdle when heated. Essential for sauces, soups, and desserts. Find at gourmet markets or make a substitute with heavy cream and buttermilk.

A classic dried herb blend from the Provence region. Typically includes thyme, rosemary, savory, oregano, marjoram, and often lavender. Ideal for seasoning grilled meats, fish, stews (like ratatouille), and roasted vegetables. Widely available in grocery stores.

A traditional strong mustard from Dijon, France, made from brown mustard seeds and verjuice (unripe grape juice). Its sharp, tangy flavor is a cornerstone in vinaigrettes, sauces (like Béchamel or Mornay), and as a condiment for meats.

Both red and white wines are fundamental in French cooking, used for deglazing pans, creating sauces (like Coq au Vin or Boeuf Bourguignon with red; Beurre Blanc with white), and poaching. Choose a decent quality wine you'd be happy to drink, as its flavor concentrates during cooking.
